# Trump’s 2025 Presidency through the Lens of Recursive Emergence Theory

President Donald Trump’s second term (beginning January 2025) can be analyzed as a complex system using **Recursive Emergence (RE)** theory. In this framework, we consider how Trump’s leadership taps into **recursive memory structures (Ψ)** (past institutional norms, media narratives, identity politics), what **emergent coherence (Φ)** his administration tries to project (a unifying national ethos or governance pattern), and how the **contradiction-resolving lattice (Ω)** of structures (legal, economic, international constraints) responds via feedback loops. We also assess whether his return brings order or disorder – evaluating **reusability (R)** of political patterns, **entropy reduction (ΔH)** (i.e. reduction of chaos), and **structural compatibility (S)** with U.S. democratic institutions. ## Recursive Memory Structures (Ψ) Reactivated or Altered

Trump’s renewed presidency has **reactivated entrenched political “memories” and precedents** – often by **restoring past policies and narratives from his first term**. Many early actions in 2025 explicitly **reinstated measures from his 2017–2021 tenure that President Biden had reversed**, creating a feedback loop with the past【37†L1-L4】. For example, on his first day back Trump reissued orders **dismantling diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs** across the federal government【10†L173-L181】, **reviving a culture-war policy** he had pursued in 2020 (and which Biden had undone). He likewise **re-imposed a ban on transgender military service** that he’d initiated previously (again overturning Biden’s reversal)【14†L431-L439】. Each of these moves resurrected contentious identity-politics battles from memory, essentially picking up old polarizing threads and amplifying them in the present.

This recursive activation of memory extends to institutions and political discourse. Trump has openly **challenged or discarded long-standing institutional norms**, altering the “memory” of what is considered customary for a president. For instance, he took the unprecedented step of **revoking or targeting the security clearances of former officials and political rivals** (including his predecessor and intelligence officers) as retribution for past dissent【13†L363-L371】 – breaking with past presidents’ reluctance to use state power against opponents. He also **pardoned hundreds of participants in the January 6, 2021 Capitol riot**【21†L7-L10】, effectively rewriting the narrative (and national memory) of that insurrection. By absolving those involved in an event widely seen as an assault on democratic process, Trump’s administration is altering how that event will be remembered and politicized, reinforcing his base’s view of them as patriots rather than criminals. 

At a broader level, Trump’s return has **upended historical reference points in U.S. policy**, triggering echoes of eras many thought closed. Internationally, he has broken from the post-1945 foreign policy consensus – for example **feuding with NATO allies and “turning on” Ukraine** support【18†L175-L181】 – in effect **challenging the collective memory of U.S.-led global order**. In sum, the Ψ of American politics – the reservoir of past narratives and institutional habits – is being aggressively **re-scripted**. Trump’s leadership is **reactivating dormant grievances and prior policy frameworks** (e.g. resurrecting border hardline measures, withdrawing from international bodies like the WHO that the prior administration had rejoined【15†L499-L507】) rather than forging consensus with more recent norms. This **cycle of action and reaction across presidencies** indicates a high degree of recurrence: policies oscillate as each administration reinstates its preferred past order, leaving the political system “stuck” in a loop of dueling memories rather than moving forward in a linear progression.

## Emergent Coherence (Φ) and Patterns of Governance

Under RE theory we examine the **emergent coherence (Φ)** of Trump’s second-term governance – essentially, what **pattern or identity** is the system organizing around, and is it stabilizing or destabilizing the whole. President Trump has forcefully projected a **nationalist, populist coherence**: in his inaugural address he vowed that America would “*flourish and be respected again*” and be “*the envy of every nation*,” declaring an end to the era of the U.S. being “*taken advantage of*”【39†L41-L49】. This rhetoric signals an emergent narrative of **national resurgence and strength** under his leadership. The administration’s swift moves – from trade tariffs to institutional shake-ups – are meant to **instantiate that “America First” vision** as the defining pattern of governance. Indeed, Trump wasted no time implementing an aggressive agenda to put U.S. interests above all else, claiming to restore past glory. 

However, the **coherence Trump seeks to impose comes at the cost of deep social schisms**, calling into question whether it truly stabilizes the broader system. The **frenetic pace and scope of his policies** have “sparked ample amounts of conflict and chaos,” pitting the U.S. against its traditional allies and injecting uncertainty into longstanding arrangements【39†L47-L55】. Rather than a single unified national identity, what has emerged is **two competing centers of coherence** in American society – his ardent supporters versus his detractors – each with their own vision. Public opinion early in the term was **evenly divided on Trump’s impact**: 41% of Americans believed his administration would improve government, while 42% believed it would make things worse【23†L163-L170】. In effect, Trump’s presidency has **crystallized two conflicting collective realities**: one cohering around his leadership as a restoration of American greatness, and another cohering in opposition, viewing his term as a threat. The emergent pattern is not a harmonious national consensus but a **polarized duality** – a stable coherence *within* each faction but instability *between* them.

On the international stage, the coherence Trump projects (an uncompromising assertion of U.S. primacy) has unsettled global systems. His administration’s actions amount to **“arguably the biggest shake-up in US foreign policy since 1945,” challenging the postwar international order**【18†L175-L181】. This includes sidelining multilateral institutions and **openly admiring authoritarian strongmen** as models, which paints a new image of America’s role abroad. While this creates a clear ideological through-line (Φ) for his foreign policy – one of transactional nationalism – it has also **destabilized alliances and norms**. Long-term partners are unsure if the U.S. will uphold prior commitments, introducing **coherence for Trump’s base (who applaud the hard-line stance) but incoherence in the global system** that had relied on U.S. leadership. 

Importantly, the **nature of Trump’s emergent coherence tends toward personalism and loyalty to himself** as the organizing principle. His governance style emphasizes **strongman authority and direct command**, aiming to align institutions under his persona. This was demonstrated by moves like **placing “self-made billionaires” and loyalists in key cabinet positions to enact his agenda**【27†L131-L139】 and reassigning civil servants to ensure policy unanimity (discussed further below). Such a top-down, personality-driven coherence can be powerful in mobilizing a base and making rapid decisions, but it runs counter to the pluralistic, institutional coherence that U.S. democracy traditionally strives for. In summary, the **Φ of Trump’s second term is a double-edged sword**: it projects a clear, forceful vision of national identity and purpose (“make America great again, again”), which **galvanizes a segment of society and the government around a unified cause**, yet it simultaneously **exacerbates fractures and uncertainty** in the wider social system, leaving the overall **social coherence fragile and prone to oscillation** rather than truly stabilized.

## Structural Constraints and the Contradiction-Resolving Lattice (Ω)

## RE Metrics: Reusability, Entropy Reduction, and Structural Compatibility

Using RE theory’s quantitative-ish metrics offers another perspective on Trump’s second term:

1. **Reusability (R):** This metric asks how **recurrent and reusable the emergent patterns are** – do Trump’s changes create templates that endure or get re-applied? In many respects, Trump’s governance shows *high reuse of past patterns*, but low long-term institutionalization. He has brought back a host of prior ideas and even drew from prepared blueprints like Heritage Foundation’s *Project 2025* policy guide. Indeed, **many of his early orders echo or directly implement proposals from the Project 2025 playbook**【37†L1-L4】, indicating that ideological frameworks developed in conservative circles are being readily reused in practice. He also proved willing to reuse his own first-term tactics (e.g. reissuing EOs he had done before, from travel bans to regulatory freezes). However, while Trump can recycle these measures, their **staying power is dubious** – because they often rely on executive fiat, a subsequent administration could just as easily undo them (as Biden did with many Trump policies, and now Trump has redone in turn). The *reusability* is largely confined to Trump-aligned actors; it does not (so far) represent a cross-partisan, durable new paradigm. In essence, **Trump’s policies are reusable within a closed loop (his base and allies)** but have not become broadly adaptive features of the U.S. political system writ large. A telling example is “Schedule F”: Trump recreated this mechanism to purge parts of the civil service【13†L381-L389】, showing *personal reuse* of a controversial idea, but if future leaders refuse to maintain it, its broader reusability in governance remains in question.

2. **Entropy Reduction (ΔH):** This metric gauges whether the administration is **reducing entropy (disorder) in the system or increasing it**. An administration that brings stability, clear rules, and consensus would have a positive ΔH (entropy reduced), whereas one that introduces chaos, unpredictability, or conflict would have a negative ΔH (entropy increased). By most accounts, Trump’s second term has **increased entropy within U.S. democratic institutions and culture**. Political scientists note a precipitous drop in perceived democratic stability: in a survey index (Bright Line Watch) rating U.S. democracy, the score **fell from 67 to 55 in the weeks after Trump’s inauguration – the largest decline recorded since the survey began in 2017**【36†L134-L142】【36†L143-L151】. This suggests a significant rise in systemic uncertainty and disorder. The administration’s rapid-fire decrees and frequent clashes with other branches have created a climate of flux. Long-standing policies (from environmental rules to foreign commitments) have been upended overnight, while agencies face internal turmoil with leadership purges. To be fair, Trump officials argue they are “dismantling bureaucracy” to streamline governance – for example, cutting regulations and entire departments could, in theory, simplify government structure (a form of entropy reduction through *compression* of complexity). And indeed, some business groups foresee a more predictable, growth-friendly environment once deregulation settles【27†L131-L139】. Yet in practice the abrupt upheavals, legal battles, and public protests signal **greater short-term chaos and information uncertainty**. Socially, entropy manifests as polarization and misinformation – phenomena that surged under Trump. Far from settling into a new equilibrium, the U.S. polity seems to be oscillating wildly, an indicator of *higher entropy*. In RE terms, Trump’s return has injected **more energy into existing fractures** than it has resolved, yielding a net ΔH that is likely positive (entropy added) – at least in the immediate horizon.

3. **Structural Compatibility (S):** This metric examines how well the new emergent order **fits with the pre-existing structural framework** – do Trump’s patterns integrate smoothly with constitutional and cultural structures, or do they clash? Here, the evidence tilts toward **low structural compatibility**. Trump’s mode of operating often conflicts with the traditional separation-of-powers and liberal democratic norms of U.S. governance. Key “load-bearing” structures – an independent judiciary, a nonpartisan civil service, free press, credible elections – have all been strained. Observers warn that **three of the seven core pillars of U.S. democracy (protected elections, rule of law, anti-corruption) are facing serious challenges under Trump’s new administration**【32†L343-L351】. For instance, Trump’s open distrust of election processes in 2024 (and his prior refusal to concede in 2020) conflict with the expectation of peaceful, system-legitimizing transitions. His extensive use of loyalty tests and retaliations (firing inspectors general, pressuring DOJ, etc.) contradict the norm of impartial rule of law. Culturally, Trump’s confrontational style and preference for divisive rhetoric (attacking the press as “fake news,” dismissing opponents as enemies) are at odds with the pluralistic, tolerant values that many American institutions promote. These mismatches have produced constant friction – a sign that **the new patterns are not naturally aligning with the old lattice**. In some cases the structure adapts (e.g. Congress or courts ultimately allow certain expansions of executive power), but often it fights back (court injunctions, bipartisan unease over norm-breaking). High *structural compatibility* would mean Trump’s changes slot into place without institutional crises, but what we observe is the opposite: **frequent institutional crises and norm violations**. Therefore, S is relatively low – Trump’s emergent order is something of an “alien element” in the system of American governance, requiring either significant structural transformation to accommodate it or resulting in continual systemic conflict.

## Comparative Insights from Prior Presidencies

A comparison to previous presidencies helps put Trump’s recursive emergence effects in perspective. **Prior administrations tended to either work within established structures or only gradually reshape them**, whereas Trump’s approach is more of a shock to the system. For example, **Barack Obama’s tenure** largely reinforced existing institutional memory and norms – he sought incremental policy progress (like the Affordable Care Act built on existing insurance frameworks) and embraced multilateral foreign policy coherence. The RE metrics for Obama might show *high S* (structural compatibility with institutions) and *moderate ΔH reduction* (a generally stable governance period with some partisan contention but no fundamental crises in norms). **Donald Trump’s first term (2017–2021)**, by contrast, introduced higher entropy with norm-breaking behaviors, yet many contradictions were resolved by checks (courts blocking overreaches, bureaucrats slow-walking policies, etc.), so the system, while rattled, contained the turmoil to a degree. Notably, Trump’s first term ended with the traumatic events of January 6, 2021, which can be seen as an extreme failure of the lattice to resolve contradictions peacefully – an eruption of latent disorder.

**The Biden administration (2021–2024)** functioned almost as a restorative interlude in RE terms: it explicitly set out to *reactivate the previous coherent state* – rejoining international agreements, rebuilding agency independence, respecting conventional norms – essentially attempting to **reduce entropy and increase structural compatibility** after Trump’s first term. Indeed, Biden re-entered frameworks like the WHO and Paris Climate Accord, shoring up the postwar international order that Trump had shaken. However, this restoration was short-lived. **Trump’s return in 2025 rapidly reversed many of Biden’s entropy-reducing moves** – for instance, Trump immediately **withdrew the U.S. from the World Health Organization again on Day 1**【15†L499-L507】, and quickly **undermined U.S. support for Ukraine and NATO that Biden had reinforced**【18†L177-L181】. This pendulum swing underscores an unstable recursive cycle: each presidency now reflexively undoes the other’s legacy. Such oscillation is itself a hallmark of a system struggling to find a new equilibrium.

Crucially, analysts note that **Trump’s second term is even more transformative (and disruptive) than his first**, suggesting a kind of acceleration in recursive effects. The **negative shift in democratic governance scores between late 2024 and early 2025 was the sharpest on record**【36†L148-L156】, indicating experts see the polity moving into markedly new (and concerning) territory. By contrast, previous second-term presidents (like Reagan, Clinton, or Obama) generally operated within a stable trajectory or even moderated in their later years, whereas Trump 2.0 has intensified conflicts. This contrast implies that **Trump’s presidency – especially in its redux – represents a break in pattern from the typical evolutionary path of modern presidencies**, which tended to build (even if contentiously) on their predecessors. Trump instead builds *against* predecessors, generating a highly volatile back-and-forth rather than a cumulative evolution. In RE theory language, the American political system under Trump is exhibiting **“recursive turbulence”**: prior emergent structures are not being steadily expanded, but rather shattered and rebuilt in opposing forms in rapid succession.

## Conclusion: Trajectory of Recursive Emergence in American Politics

Donald Trump’s second term appears to be a **critical inflection point in America’s recursive political evolution**, one that in many ways **accelerates destabilizing feedback loops and reverses certain long-term emergent trends**. Rather than guiding the system toward a new stable order, Trump’s leadership has thus far amplified oscillations in the body politic. **Entropy within institutions and civic life has increased**, as evidenced by rising polarization, institutional conflicts, and expert assessments that the U.S. is **“moving swiftly from liberal democracy toward some form of authoritarianism,” according to a vast majority of political scientists**【36†L134-L142】. This suggests a *backwards step* in the evolution of democratic coherence – a partial unraveling of the tight norms and expectations that previously governed political conduct. The **re-activation of divisive memories (Ψ)** and the formation of antagonistic coherent camps (Φ) have driven up social and political disorder (negative ΔH). 

At the same time, one could argue Trump’s tumultuous approach is **accelerating a kind of meta-emergence** – forcing the nation to confront contradictions that had simmered under the surface. By pushing institutions to their breaking points, his presidency could catalyze reforms or realignments (for better or worse) that a more complacent administration might not. In that sense, the recursive feedback is *sped up*: changes that might have taken decades (such as reasserting executive power, or questioning the role of America globally) are being compressed into a short span. Whether this ultimately produces a higher-order stability or just further chaos remains to be seen. What is clear is that **Trump’s return has injected a powerful jolt into the evolutionary process** – one observer described it as *a “vibe shift” in the global order* and noted that after achieving “the greatest comeback in political history,” Trump **“thinks he can do anything”**【40†L1-L4】. That confidence has translated into an administration unafraid to break molds and defy constraints, which accelerates the pace of confrontations across the system.

In net effect, **Trump’s second term is tending to stall or even roll back aspects of recursive emergence** that characterized the post-war and post-Cold War American trajectory (such as steadily expanding liberal democratic norms and international integration). Instead, it **propels the system onto a more volatile path**, where emergent patterns are quickly challenged and negated in a recursive loop. Unless counter-balancing forces in the Ω lattice can establish a new equilibrium (for example, a constitutional recommitment to checks and balances, or a public consensus rejecting extremism), the **American political system may remain in a high-entropy cycle**. In RE theory terms, Trump’s presidency so far **embodies a phase of increased fluctuation and uncertainty**, rather than convergence to order. It accelerates the feedback cycles of polarization and institutional stress, and by doing so, it **risks a form of devolution** – a reversal to more primitive governance dynamics (personalist rule, friend-enemy politics) that undercut the complex emergent order of a pluralistic democracy. 

Ultimately, the coming years will test whether this disruptive recursion leads to **creative emergence or systemic breakdown**. Will the heightened contradictions give birth to new, more resilient structures (perhaps reforms strengthening democracy, or a realigned party system), or will they produce a lasting decline in institutional integrity? For now, the indicators (R, ΔH, S) tilt negative: Trump’s second term has **low reusability beyond its faction, high entropy, and poor compatibility with existing structures**, all of which point to a **destabilizing and regressive phase** in U.S. political evolution【36†L143-L151】. In plain terms, Trump’s return has accelerated America into a turbulent loop – one that has stalled forward progress and begun to unwind some of the hard-earned emergent properties of its democracy. The challenge for the American system will be to absorb this turbulence and find a new steady state for the next stage of its recursive emergence.
